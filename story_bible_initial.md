## World

### The Coast

A stretch of shoreline in an unnamed country, temperate climate. Once there were fishing villages, a ferry terminal, a coast guard station. Now there is rock, sea, and wind. The towns didn't die — they simply emptied. Quietly. Over years. No catastrophe. Just the slow retreat of relevance.

The government agency that manages coastal infrastructure still exists, technically. But its letters stopped coming. The supply boat hasn't appeared in six months. The radio plays static. Somewhere in a ministry basement, a file marked "Lighthouse Station Fourteen" is gathering dust.

### The Lighthouse

An old tower — stone, iron, and glass. Built in a style that suggests the early 20th century but could be older. It has:

- **The lamp room** (top): The Fresnel lens, the fuel system, the signal lamp. This is the heart.
- **The watch room** (below lamp): The telegraph, the log desk, the weather instruments. This is where signals arrive.
- **The living quarters** (middle): A cot, a table, a stove, a shelf of books. Sparse. Functional.
- **The store room** (base): Fuel drums, spare parts, tools. The supply that isn't being resupplied.
- **The exterior**: The gallery (walkway around the lamp room), the cliff path, the shore access.

The lighthouse is a character. It ages. It breaks. It demands care. What the player does to it — and what they let decay — tells a story.

### The Sea

The sea is constant, vast, and indifferent. It is also, in the game's thematic framework, a **metaphor for memory** — deep, unnavigable, and containing things that surface when you stop looking.

The player never enters the sea. They never see beneath its surface. The sea exists as horizon, sound, and signal source.

## Characters

### The Keeper

| Attribute | Detail |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Name** | Not revealed. Referred to as "the keeper" or "you." |
| **Age** | Mid-40s to early 50s (implied) |
| **Background** | Former maritime professional. Precise role TBD — options: signals officer, harbor pilot, marine biologist. Decision deferred to M2. |
| **Personality** | Methodical. Taciturn. Finds comfort in routine. Writes logbook entries the way other people breathe — automatically, without thinking about it. |
| **Want** | To do the job. To keep the light alive. To not think about what came before. |
| **Need** | To face what they came here to escape. To accept that the light was never for the ships. |
| **Fear** | That no one is coming. That the light doesn't matter. That staying was not duty — it was hiding. |

**Voice**: Terse. Functional. No poetry, no introspection. The keeper writes like someone filling out a form — until the form starts cracking. Late-game logbook entries trail off. Words are crossed out. Dates repeat.

### The Signals (not a character — a presence)

The signals are not a person. They are not an entity. They are more like an **echo** — of the coast's maritime past, of the keeper's own history, of something that occupies the space between memory and sea.

The signals never identify themselves. They never explain. They only transmit.

## Narrative Structure

### Act 1 — The Routine (Days 1–5)

The keeper maintains the lighthouse. The signals are normal — SOS calls, patrol check-ins, weather reports. The world is familiar. The player learns the systems, the space, the rhythm.

Underneath: small anomalies. A signal that uses a ship name from 40 years ago. A bearing that doesn't match any shipping lane. A weather report for a storm that never arrives.

**Player state**: Curious but functional.

### Act 2 — The Corruption (Days 6–10)

The signals change. They include words that shouldn't be there — a name the keeper knows, a date from the past. The Morse degrades. Some signals seem to respond to what the keeper did during the day.

The logbook starts showing cracks. An entry is crossed out. A page is torn. The keeper writes the same date twice.

**Player state**: Uncertain. The rules are shifting.

### Act 3 — The Reckoning (Days 11–14)

The signals are no longer asking for help. They are offering answers — to questions the keeper hasn't asked aloud. The lighthouse is failing. The keeper must choose what to maintain and what to release.

The final signals point inward: not "what is out there?" but "why did you stay?"

**Player state**: Accepting. The mystery resolves not as plot revelation but as emotional understanding.

## Backstory (Internal — Not Revealed to Player Directly)

> *This section is for internal studio use. The player never reads this directly. They piece it together from fragments.*

Before the lighthouse, the keeper was [ROLE TBD] in the coastal town. They had a partner — someone who worked on the water. There was an incident — not a dramatic shipwreck, but something quieter. A departure that didn't end in return. A radio call that went unanswered.

The keeper took the lighthouse posting because it was the last place they felt close to that person. The light is ostensibly for ships. But the keeper keeps the light on because some part of them still believes it will bring someone home.

The signals, in the late game, begin to echo the language of that final radio call. Not literally — but in rhythm, in word choice, in the way they break mid-sentence.

**This is the emotional core**: the lighthouse is a vigil. The keeper doesn't know if they're mourning or waiting. The game never tells them — or the player — which one it is.

## Signal Taxonomy

| Type | Behavior | Frequency |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Genuine** | Standard maritime signal. Outcome at dawn is verifiable. | ~33% of all signals |
| **Ambiguous** | Signal is plausible but unverifiable. Dawn reveals partial or unclear outcomes. | ~50% of all signals |
| **False** | Signal contains impossible information. Dawn reveals nothing — or something worse than nothing. | ~17% of all signals |

### Anchor Rule (from CONV-2026-001)

At least 1 in 3 signals must be an **anchor** — a signal with a clear, verifiable consequence at dawn. This gives the player enough feedback to trust the system without removing the ambiguity that makes it meaningful.

## Tone Guide

### Register

The game's written content operates in **maintenance register** — field-report language. Short sentences. Passive observations. No editorializing.

### Examples by context

**Logbook entry (early)**:
> Day 3. 0620h. Lens cleaned. Fuel at 64%. Wind NW, moderate. No activity overnight. Signal received 0347h, standard SOS, bearing NE. Responded. Wreckage sighted at dawn, northeast rocks. No survivors visible. Logged.

**Logbook entry (late)**:
> Day 11. Time unclear. Lens ~~cleaned~~ Something on the glass. Not dirt. Fuel at fuel at. The signal said 0347h said. Same time as. Bearing. The same bearing.

**Signal (genuine)**:
> ... --- ... / -- .- -.-- -.. .- -.-- / -... . .- .-. .. -. --. / -. .  
> SOS — MAYDAY — BEARING NE  
> Vessel identification: MV AURORA. Crew of four. Requesting light.

**Signal (false, late-game)**:
> -.- . . .--. . .-. / -.-- --- ..- / .-.. . ..-. - / - .... . / .-.. .- -- .--. / --- -.  
> KEEPER. YOU LEFT THE LAMP ON. WHO ARE YOU WAITING FOR?

## Sensitivity Framework

### Themes requiring care

| Theme | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Isolation** | A condition, not a punishment. The keeper chose to stay. That choice is respected. |
| **Loss** | Implied through absence — the empty chair, the second cup. Never depicted graphically. |
| **Grief** | Shown as the *shape* of what's missing, not the event that caused it. |
| **Mental deterioration** | The logbook degradation is phenomenological, not clinical. We show *what changes*, not *diagnose why*. |

### Content note (launch)

> *"This game explores themes of solitude, memory, and what we leave behind. There is no violence or self-harm. Some players may find the themes resonate with personal experiences of loss."*

### Review protocol
